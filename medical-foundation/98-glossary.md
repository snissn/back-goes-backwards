<div class="glossary-section">
  <h1 id="ref-glossary">Glossary</h1>
  <p><strong>Alleviating Compression</strong> — The act of reducing pressure on tissues and systems that are under chronic stress, often through engaging the posterior chain.</p>
  <p><strong>Anterior Loading</strong> — An overreliance on the body’s anterior structures, such as lumbar discs, hip flexors, and abdominal fascia, leading to imbalances and systemic effects.</p>
  <p><strong>Ashtanga Yoga</strong> — A dynamic form of yoga that emphasizes alignment, spinal elongation, and posterior chain engagement.</p>
  <p><strong>Autoimmune Disorders</strong> — Conditions where the immune system mistakenly attacks the body, potentially exacerbated by mechanical dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>Balanced Force Distribution</strong> — Ensuring that mechanical forces are evenly spread across the body to prevent strain and dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>Biomechanical</strong> — Relating to the mechanical aspects of living organisms, particularly the forces exerted by muscles and gravity on the skeletal structure.</p>
  <p><strong>Biomechanical-Based Medicine</strong> — An approach that incorporates biomechanics, physics, and engineering principles into the understanding and treatment of health and disease.</p>
  <p><strong>Bladder Meridian</strong> — A yang meridian governing the back of the body, supporting structural integrity.</p>
  <p><strong>Breathing</strong> — The process of inhaling and exhaling, which serves as a vital link between the body's structural framework and energetic flow in both Mechanical-Based Medicine and TCM.</p>
  <p><strong>Chronic Conditions</strong> — Long-term health issues that can arise from mechanical dysfunction, including pain, autoimmune inflammation, hormonal dysregulation, and circulatory inefficiencies.</p>
  <p><strong>Chronic Fatigue Syndrome</strong> — A complex condition characterized by persistent fatigue and systemic inefficiencies, exacerbated by mechanical dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)</strong> — A debilitating condition marked by profound exhaustion, cognitive difficulties, and physical symptoms, often exacerbated by mechanical inefficiencies.</p>
  <p><strong>Circulatory Dysfunction</strong> — Impairment of blood flow due to mechanical dysfunction, often resulting in conditions like venous insufficiency and fluid retention.</p>
  <p><strong>Du Meridian (Governing Vessel)</strong> — A primary meridian in TCM that runs along the spine and governs yang energy, vital for health and vitality.</p>
  <p><strong>Dynamic Stability</strong> — The ability of the body to maintain balance and control during movement, particularly in weight-bearing joints.</p>
  <p><strong>Fibromyalgia</strong> — A condition involving widespread pain and fatigue, often linked to mechanical inefficiencies and anterior loading.</p>
  <p><strong>Gall Bladder Meridian</strong> — A meridian responsible for detoxification and side-body flexibility.</p>
  <p><strong>Holistic Approach</strong> — A comprehensive framework that integrates various practices to address mechanical dysfunction and promote overall health.</p>
  <p><strong>Jalandhara Bandha (Throat Lock)</strong> — An energetic lock that aligns the cervical spine and regulates energy flow to the brain.</p>
  <p><strong>Joint Misalignment</strong> — A consequence of mechanical imbalance where forces acting on the body destabilize key joints, leading to uneven wear and potential degeneration.</p>
  <p><strong>Large Intestine Meridian</strong> — A meridian extending from the arms into the head and torso, associated with digestion and respiration.</p>
  <p><strong>Lateral Axis</strong> — The axis of the spine that allows for side flexion, important for stability, flexibility, and balanced posture.</p>
  <p><strong>Lung Meridian</strong> — A meridian essential for breath and systemic balance.</p>
  <p><strong>Lymphedema</strong> — Swelling due to impaired lymphatic drainage, which can be influenced by mechanical factors such as alignment and compression.</p>
  <p><strong>Macrocosmic Orbit</strong> — A cyclical flow of Qi that integrates inhalation and exhalation with the spine's three axes in practices like Yoga.</p>
  <p><strong>Mechanical Dysfunction</strong> — A root cause of many chronic conditions resulting from the body’s inability to distribute forces evenly across its structures.</p>
  <p><strong>Mechanical-Based Medicine</strong> — A paradigm that focuses on correcting mechanical dysfunctions to improve systemic health outcomes.</p>
  <p><strong>Mechanical-Based Medicine (MBM)</strong> — A modern discipline that elucidates how mechanical dysfunctions—imbalances in posture, joint alignment, and muscular activation—are central to many chronic and systemic health issues.</p>
  <p><strong>Meridians</strong> — An extensive network of pathways in the body through which Qi circulates, playing a crucial role in health according to TCM.</p>
  <p><strong>Mula Bandha (Root Lock)</strong> — An energetic lock located at the pelvic floor that stabilizes the pelvis and grounds the body's energy.</p>
  <p><strong>Musculoskeletal Disorders</strong> — Conditions affecting the spine, joints, muscles, and connective tissues, often originating from mechanical dysfunctions and leading to pain and reduced mobility.</p>
  <p><strong>Ordinary and Extraordinary Meridians</strong> — Ordinary meridians are regular pathways for Qi flow, while extraordinary meridians are deeper pathways that integrate physical structure and energetic flow.</p>
  <p><strong>Pelvic Alignment</strong> — The positioning of the pelvis in a neutral position to maintain balance and distribute mechanical forces evenly.</p>
  <p><strong>Pelvic Congestion Syndrome</strong> — A condition characterized by chronic pelvic pain linked to venous insufficiency and mechanical dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>Pelvic Congestion Syndrome (PCS)</strong> — A condition characterized by chronic pelvic pain due to blood pooling in the pelvic veins, often linked to biomechanical issues.</p>
  <p><strong>Peng</strong> — A buoyant quality in Tai Chi that provides structural integrity and stability through an outward, expansive energy.</p>
  <p><strong>Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)</strong> — A hormonal disorder that can be exacerbated by biomechanical dysfunction in the pelvic region.</p>
  <p><strong>Posterior Chain</strong> — A group of muscles and connective tissues along the back of the body, crucial for maintaining alignment and distributing forces.</p>
  <p><strong>Posterior Loading</strong> — A framework for reactivating and strengthening the posterior chain, which includes the glutes, hamstrings, spinal stabilizers, and associated fascia, to address mechanical dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>Qi</strong> — The vital life force or energy that flows through the body, essential for sustaining health.</p>
  <p><strong>Redistribution of Mechanical Forces</strong> — The process of shifting mechanical stress from overburdened structures to those designed for load-bearing and shock absorption.</p>
  <p><strong>Rheumatoid arthritis (RA)</strong> — A chronic autoimmune condition characterized by persistent joint inflammation, pain, and systemic fatigue.</p>
  <p><strong>Rotational Axis</strong> — The axis of the spine that allows for internal and external rotation, essential for maintaining core stability and distributing mechanical forces.</p>
  <p><strong>Spinal Compression</strong> — A condition where the lumbar and cervical regions are compressed due to anterior loading, leading to reduced shock absorption and integrity of intervertebral discs.</p>
  <p><strong>Spinal Elongation</strong> — The practice of maintaining the spine's natural length and curvature to reduce compression and improve overall biomechanics.</p>
  <p><strong>Spleen Meridian</strong> — A meridian that supports nourishment and internal energy distribution.</p>
  <p><strong>Stomach Meridian</strong> — A meridian associated with digestion, flowing along the legs and torso.</p>
  <p><strong>Strength Training</strong> — A form of exercise focusing on building resilience and functional strength, particularly in the posterior chain.</p>
  <p><strong>Symmetry Restoration</strong> — The process of reestablishing balanced movements and forces across the body to prevent wear and injury.</p>
  <p><strong>Synthesis of Motion</strong> — A framework that integrates insights from biomechanics and energy systems into a unified theory of health and movement.</p>
  <p><strong>Systemic Conditions</strong> — Health issues that affect the entire body, often exacerbated by mechanical dysfunction, such as autoimmune disorders and chronic fatigue.</p>
  <p><strong>Tai Chi</strong> — A practice involving slow, deliberate movements that promote balance, alignment, and the engagement of the posterior chain.</p>
  <p><strong>Three-Dimensional Spinal Motion</strong> — The ability of the spine to move in three primary dimensions: flexion-extension, rotation, and lateral flexion.</p>
  <p><strong>Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)</strong> — A holistic approach that integrates principles of energetic flow with structural health, emphasizing the body's meridian systems.</p>
  <p><strong>Triple Burner Meridian</strong> — A meridian that harmonizes emotional balance and circulatory flow.</p>
  <p><strong>Uddiyana Bandha (Abdominal Lock)</strong> — An energetic lock that directs energy upward and decompresses the lumbar spine.</p>
  <p><strong>Venous Insufficiency</strong> — A condition marked by impaired blood flow from the lower limbs to the heart, often exacerbated by mechanical dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>Vertical Axis</strong> — The axis of the spine that allows for flexion and extension, crucial for balance, stability, and energetic flow.</p>
  <p><strong>alignment</strong> — The proper positioning of the body's structures to ensure optimal function and efficiency.</p>
  <p><strong>anterior loading</strong> — A condition where prolonged sitting and poor posture shift strain away from the back muscles to weaker structures in the front, leading to mechanical dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>autoimmune diseases</strong> — Conditions in which the immune system mistakenly attacks the body's own tissues, often exacerbated by biomechanical dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>axial rotation</strong> — Twisting of the spine around its vertical axis, important for dynamic movements.</p>
  <p><strong>biological organism</strong> — A living entity characterized by biological processes, including cellular functions and metabolic activities.</p>
  <p><strong>biomechanical dysfunction</strong> — Disruptions in the body's mechanical systems that lead to pain, inefficiency, and systemic health issues.</p>
  <p><strong>breathwork</strong> — Practices that focus on conscious breathing to harmonize physical structure and energy flow.</p>
  <p><strong>carpal tunnel syndrome</strong> — A condition caused by compression of the median nerve in the wrist, often linked to repetitive strain and poor ergonomics.</p>
  <p><strong>chronic inflammation</strong> — A prolonged inflammatory response that can lead to tissue damage and various health issues.</p>
  <p><strong>chronic low back pain</strong> — Persistent pain in the lower back often resulting from poor alignment and muscular imbalances.</p>
  <p><strong>diaphragmatic breathing</strong> — A breathing technique that enhances lung capacity and supports lymphatic drainage.</p>
  <p><strong>disc herniation</strong> — A condition where the soft, gel-like center of a spinal disc pushes through its outer layer, potentially compressing nearby nerves.</p>
  <p><strong>dynamic stability</strong> — The ability to maintain balance and alignment during movement, supported by the engagement of the posterior chain.</p>
  <p><strong>energy inefficiency</strong> — The excessive energy expenditure required to perform basic tasks due to compromised biomechanics.</p>
  <p><strong>erector spinae</strong> — Muscles responsible for extending the spine and resisting forward flexion, crucial for maintaining an upright posture.</p>
  <p><strong>ergonomic adjustments</strong> — Changes made to workspaces or tools to improve posture and reduce strain.</p>
  <p><strong>facet joints</strong> — Small joints in the spine that guide movement and maintain alignment, vulnerable to asymmetrical loading.</p>
  <p><strong>flexion-extension</strong> — Movements involving bending forward and backward, essential for various physical activities.</p>
  <p><strong>force distribution</strong> — The way forces are spread across the body's structures during movement, which affects overall stability and health.</p>
  <p><strong>gluteus maximus</strong> — One of the body's most powerful muscles, countering anterior pelvic tilt and decreasing strain on the lumbar spine.</p>
  <p><strong>hamstrings</strong> — Muscles that provide dynamic stability to both the hips and knees, essential for movement efficiency.</p>
  <p><strong>lateral axis</strong> — The axis that encompasses side flexion, supporting side-body flexibility and stability.</p>
  <p><strong>lateral flexion</strong> — Bending of the spine to one side, a motion that requires stability to prevent collapse.</p>
  <p><strong>load redistribution</strong> — The process of managing mechanical forces to protect structures and function efficiently, central to the concept of posterior loading.</p>
  <p><strong>localized stress and damage</strong> — The concentration of stress on specific areas of the body due to biomechanical dysfunction, leading to pain and injury.</p>
  <p><strong>lower limb support structures</strong> — Components including the gastrocnemius, soleus, and Achilles tendon that absorb impact during movement and act as levers for propulsion.</p>
  <p><strong>lymphedema</strong> — Swelling due to the accumulation of lymphatic fluid, often resulting from mechanical disruptions in the lymphatic system.</p>
  <p><strong>mechanical dysfunction</strong> — A failure in the body's mechanical systems that can lead to stress, pain, and systemic health problems.</p>
  <p><strong>mechanical foundation</strong> — The structural support that underpins the body's biological systems, including alignment, force distribution, and structural dynamics.</p>
  <p><strong>mechanical system</strong> — A system that operates based on physical laws and principles, particularly in relation to movement and force.</p>
  <p><strong>movement patterns</strong> — The habitual ways in which the body moves, which can contribute to biomechanical dysfunction if inefficient.</p>
  <p><strong>multifidus</strong> — A muscle that stabilizes individual vertebrae and contributes to spinal stability.</p>
  <p><strong>musculoskeletal disorders</strong> — Conditions that affect the muscles, bones, and joints, often resulting from biomechanical dysfunction.</p>
  <p><strong>osteoarthritis</strong> — A degenerative joint disease often initiated by uneven loading and misalignment of joints.</p>
  <p><strong>pelvic and hip stabilizers</strong> — Muscles, particularly the gluteus maximus and hamstrings, that generate force for movement while ensuring proper alignment of the pelvis and lower back.</p>
  <p><strong>plantar fasciitis</strong> — A condition involving inflammation of the plantar fascia, often caused by poor foot biomechanics.</p>
  <p><strong>posterior chain</strong> — The group of muscles located along the back of the body, including the glutes, hamstrings, and spinal stabilizers, that are crucial for maintaining proper alignment and movement.</p>
  <p><strong>posterior loading</strong> — A condition where the net mechanical force flows through the posterior side of the spine and its associated muscles, optimizing physical stability and systemic health.</p>
  <p><strong>rotational axis</strong> — The axis that facilitates internal and external rotation, engaging core stability and redistributing forces along the spine.</p>
  <p><strong>sciatica</strong> — Pain that radiates along the path of the sciatic nerve, typically caused by compression or irritation in the lower back.</p>
  <p><strong>spinal realignment</strong> — The process of correcting the position of the spine to improve function and reduce pain.</p>
  <p><strong>spinal resilience</strong> — The ability to maintain alignment and functionality over time, crucial for overall health.</p>
  <p><strong>spinal stabilizers</strong> — Muscles that safeguard the integrity and mobility of the vertebral column, including the erector spinae, multifidus, and thoracolumbar fascia.</p>
  <p><strong>strain energy</strong> — The energy stored in a material under deformation, which can lead to pain and degeneration if excessive.</p>
  <p><strong>structural dynamics</strong> — The behavior and interaction of various body structures during movement and load-bearing activities.</p>
  <p><strong>symmetry</strong> — The balanced distribution of forces across the spine, essential for preventing degeneration and maintaining health.</p>
  <p><strong>systemic health</strong> — The overall physiological efficiency and functioning of the body, influenced by mechanical alignment and movement.</p>
  <p><strong>systemic processes</strong> — The overall functions and interactions of different bodily systems that maintain health and homeostasis.</p>
  <p><strong>thoracic outlet syndrome</strong> — A condition caused by compression of nerves and blood vessels between the collarbone and first rib, often related to poor posture.</p>
  <p><strong>thoracolumbar fascia</strong> — A dense connective tissue that anchors the posterior chain to the core, supporting spinal stability.</p>
  <p><strong>three-dimensional motion</strong> — Movement within the three primary axes: vertical, rotational, and lateral, essential for maintaining health.</p>
  <p><strong>upper-body stabilizers</strong> — Muscles such as the trapezius, rhomboids, and posterior deltoids that maintain upright posture and shoulder stability.</p>
  <p><strong>varicose veins</strong> — Swollen, twisted veins that can occur due to poor circulation and increased pressure in the veins.</p>
  <p><strong>venous insufficiency</strong> — A condition in which veins struggle to return blood to the heart, often exacerbated by poor alignment and biomechanics.</p>
  <p><strong>vertical axis</strong> — The axis encompassing flexion and extension, central to stability and elongation of the spine.</p>
